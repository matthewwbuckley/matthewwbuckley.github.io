On Color Palettes

  • colour-theory
  • design
  • productivity
  • js

Bought a new book on color theory today. There’s something meditative about limiting yourself to five colors. Constraints breed creativity.

The book is Josef Albers’ “Interaction of Color.” Published in 1963, still relevant today. His core insight: we never see colors in isolation. Every hue is influenced by its neighbors.

Put a gray square on a yellow background and it looks purple. Put the same gray on a purple background and it looks yellow. The color itself hasn’t changed—only the context.

This is why I’ve been obsessing over palettes. Four colors, carefully chosen, can create the illusion of dozens. Dark backgrounds make light text pop. Accent colors sing when they’re rare.

I’ve settled on a formula: background, foreground, and two accents. The background sets the mood—warm or cool, light or dark. The foreground handles text. The accents do the heavy lifting of visual interest.

Each palette gets a name. Sunset. Forest. Ocean. Mono. Berry. They’re not just colors; they’re atmospheres. A post about calm productivity might get the ocean palette. Something more urgent gets sunset.

The constraint is freeing. When you can’t reach for any color, you learn which ones actually matter.
